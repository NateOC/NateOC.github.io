Quarter Pipe

Overview

This was my first major woodworking project, connecting with my love of skateboarding. I wanted to build a quarter pipe for practicing tricks at home/at school, and decided to design and build it myself. I researched different designs and materials, and settled on a simple yet sturdy structure using plywood and two by fours. The school had a lot of two by fours laying around, so I was able to build this pretty affordably. The process involved sketching the height and transition I wanted on the ramp, then measuring and cutting the wood, assembling the frame, and attaching the plywood surface. It was a challenging but rewarding experience, that took much longer than I expected. I learned a lot about woodworking techniques and safety along the way.

Making the Frame

I spent a good amount of time thinking about the transition, cause I wanted the ramp to get close to vert (vertical) but still be reasonably small so I could learn new tricks on it. I decided on a 4-foot tall, 6-foot wide quarter pipe that had a rib cage like frame. I sketched out the design on graph paper to help visualize the dimensions and shape. Once I had the design finalized, I gathered the materials I needed, and started by cutting the two by fours to the appropriate lengths. I then scaled my sketch of the transition up, and drew it onto the plywood sheets to use as a cutting guide. After cutting out the transition with a jigsaw, I cut knotches out of the middle sheet of plywood, that the two by fours would fit into. This added support in multiple directions, and I even added some diagonal bracing on the middle piece to stop it from flexing. Next, I assembled the frame using screws to attach the two by fours to the plywood sheets. At this stage, the ramp looked like this:

Adding the Surface and Coping

With the frame complete, I moved on to attaching the plywood surface. I decided to use 1/4" marine plywood for the surface, combining flexibility and strength. Conveniently, the length of the ramp's transition was exactly 8ft, so I could use the full length of the plywood sheets without needing to cut them down. I did have to cut one sheet in half, to cover the width of the ramp. I carefully aligned the plywood sheets on top of the frame, ensuring that they were flush with the edges, and screwed them into the two by fours underneath. I made sure to space the screws evenly to provide a secure attachment while avoiding splitting the plywood. Once the plywood surface was securely attached, I used wood putty to cover the screws, making sure the ramp had a smooth surface. Next, I added coping to the top edge of the ramp. Coping is a metal pipe that provides a smooth edge for grinding and lip tricks. I measured and cut a length of metal pipe to fit the width of the ramp, but realized I didn't have anywhere to attach the coping. I added two extra two by fours to the top of the ramp, one on the front and one on the back, to give the coping something to be screwed into. I drilled holes through the coping and into the wooden supports, then secured it with screws and washers to ensure a tight fit. Takeaways

Overall, building this quarter pipe was a challenging but rewarding experience. I learned a lot about woodworking techniques, safety, and problem-solving throughout the process. The ramp quickly became a source of pride and enjoyment for me, and I'm glad I put the time and effort into building it. This project is what inspired me to take on more woodworking projects in the future.
