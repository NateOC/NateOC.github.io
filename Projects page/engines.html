Making Model Rocket Engines

Overview

After looking deeper into model rockets, and becoming more independent in my construction, I realized I should be making my own engines. The chemistry was simple enough, all I needed was a fuel (sorbitol) and an oxidizer (Potassium Nitrate). I looked into ways to improve this fuel as well, like adding iron oxide to make it burn more quickly, or glycerol to make the mixture easier to pour. I haven't tried these additives yet, but I plan to in the very near future. I talked with my former chemistry teacher to see what materials he had, and he was able to provide me with Potassium Nitrate and a safe lab space to work in. I ordered sorbitol online, and got to work.

Process

The first step was to grind the Potassium Nitrate and the sorbitol into a fine powder, and then mix them together at a 65:35 ratio. My research showed me that sorbitol was best because it melted at such a low temperature, and I wouldn't have to worry about caramelization like with regular sugar.

Once I had the powder mixed in a beaker (this was a small test batch), I placed it on a hot plate and heated it to about 150 degrees Celsius. The sorbitol melted and combined with the Potassium Nitrate to form a thick, white paste. I stirred it constantly to ensure an even consistency.

Once the mixture was ready, I poured it onto a piece of tin foil for testing.

Stirring the Mixture

This is what my setup looked like: using a hot plate and a popsicle stick to stir so there's no risk of sparks. I sat stirring like this for a while. I didn't want to heat it too quickly. I made sure to be extra safe, wearing gloves and goggles at all times.

Final Mixture

This is a picture of the fuel mixture after it was poured onto the tin foil. Once it cooled down it took on a much more white color and a firm texture. Even just after pouring, I could feel it solidifying quickly.

Results

This is still a work in progress, and I haven't launched any of the engines I've made yet. For now, I just have a video of the fuel burning on the tin foil. It burned with a steady flame, and produced a ton of smoke. I'm excited to try this fuel in an actual engine casing soon, and try adding iron oxide to speed up the burn.

Next Steps

My next steps are to try adding additives to the fuel mixture, and see how they affect the burn rate and thrust. I also need to design and build some engine casings to hold the fuel, and test them in actual launches. I want to add nozzles to the engines, using one of the CNC machines at school to make them, improving thrust. I would most likely make these out of aluminum, as that is what we have in class, but I fear this may be too heavy. I'll document my progress here as I continue to experiment.
